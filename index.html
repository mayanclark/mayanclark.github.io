Why I do this work

The tech gap is an
environmental justice issue.

Satellite imagery can show which neighborhoods are losing tree canopy fastest. Watershed models can predict where flooding will worsen. GIS can map who bears the heaviest pollution burden. This information exists — but it's often locked behind expensive software licenses, academic paywalls, or technical jargon that puts it out of reach for the communities who need it most.

I got into geospatial work because I saw that gap firsthand. My research on urban tree canopy in Baltimore revealed stark inequities in who has access to green space — and who gets left out of the decisions that shape it. My work at the Chesapeake Bay Foundation showed me how powerful a map can be when it's made for a community, not just about one.

I'm building a practice that bridges that gap — making NASA-grade environmental analysis accessible, affordable, and actionable for nonprofits, small farms, advocacy groups, and underserved communities working to protect their own land and water.

"The communities most impacted by environmental harm are often the last to have access to the tools that prove it."

About me

Geographer, analyst,
and land advocate.

I'm Maya Clark — a geospatial analyst and environmental researcher with a B.S. in Geography and Environmental Planning (GIS minor) from Towson University. My work sits at the intersection of satellite remote sensing, watershed science, and community-centered advocacy.

I've led urban heat island research at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, mapped sea level rise and coastal erosion for the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, analyzed fisheries data across 12 West African nations for CIEL, and produced award-winning research on urban tree canopy equity in Baltimore City.

I'm also rooted in regenerative agriculture — applying GIS to map growing zones and water resources at Sacred Roots Herbal Sanctuary in Shepherdstown, WV, and developing grant funding strategies to support small farms and land stewardship projects like it.

Published research

CLA Summer Research Grant 2025 · AAG Mid-Atlantic Best Paper Award · $250

Urban Canopy Equity: Assessing Socioeconomic Disparities in Tree Cover and Quantifying Urban Forest Changes in Baltimore

This study analyzes 11 years (2011–2021) of tree canopy health and distribution in Baltimore City using Landsat imagery, U.S. Forest Service TCC data, and U.S. Census sociodemographic variables. Findings revealed a 17% decline in Baltimore's percent tree canopy cover, with statistically significant loss concentrated in the northwestern quadrant. Stepwise regression identified population density and vacant housing as the strongest negative predictors of canopy cover — exposing the environmental justice dimensions of urban greening and informing targeted reforestation strategies for communities most at risk. Methods included Mann-Kendall trend analysis, Global Moran's I, and Getis-Ord Gi* hot spot analysis in ArcGIS Pro. Faculty mentor: Dr. Carter Wang.
